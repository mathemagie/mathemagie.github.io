Asstral Traveler

A Sex Toy to Connect with Deep Time

Artist: Thomas Hämén
Year: 2019
Exhibition: Artissima Art Fair, Turin
Material: Coprolite (fossilized dinosaur feces)

What Is the Asstral Traveler?

The Asstral Traveler is a provocative artwork by Thomas Hämén that exists at the intersection of paleontology, intimacy, and conceptual art. It is an anal stimulation device sculpted from authentic coprolite—fossilized feces from dinosaurs that roamed Earth approximately 140 million years ago.

~140,000,000 years

The Concept: Deep Time and Human Connection

At its core, this work explores the concept of "deep time"—geological timeframes that extend far beyond human comprehension. How do we, as humans with lifespans measured in decades, connect with events that occurred across millions of years?

"Connecting a sex toy with the faeces of an extinct animal humorously links the human interfaces for excrement and pleasure, with the vestigial experience—known but felt by no one—that dinosaurs once existed on earth."

Hämén's approach is both playful and profound. By using one of the most intimate human experiences—sexual pleasure—as a bridge to prehistoric existence, the work creates a visceral, bodily connection to deep time. This isn't abstract contemplation; it's a physical, intimate encounter with ancient matter.

The Title: Asstral Travel

Etymology & Meaning

The title "Asstral Travel" is a clever wordplay on "astral travel" (also known as astral projection), an ancient belief that human consciousness can separate from the physical body to journey through the universe. Here, the journey is both literal—through the body—and temporal—through deep geological time.

The Material: Coprolite

Coprolite is far more than fossilized excrement. In paleontology, it holds significant scientific value, revealing crucial information about:

  • Ancient animals' diets and eating habits
  • Digestive system structures and functions
  • Prehistoric ecosystems and habitats
  • Environmental conditions of extinct eras

By transforming this scientific artifact into an object of pleasure, Hämén collapses the distance between clinical observation and intimate experience. The coprolite becomes a time capsule that you don't just study—you physically engage with.

The Human Interfaces: Excrement and Pleasure

One of the most striking aspects of this work is how it links two fundamental human bodily functions that share the same anatomical interface but serve vastly different purposes. This duality mirrors the dual nature of coprolite itself: once waste, now a window into the past.

The Paradox of Presence

Dinosaurs existed. We know this intellectually through fossils, scientific evidence, and reconstructions. Yet their existence is "felt by no one"—no living being has experienced their presence. The Asstral Traveler attempts to bridge this gap through the most primal form of knowing: physical sensation.

Context & Reception

The work was discovered at the Artissima art fair in Turin, Italy, in November 2019. As with many provocative artworks, it challenges viewers to confront their discomfort with bodily functions, sexuality, and the absurdity of our attempts to grasp geological time scales.

Questions the Work Raises

How do we truly connect with deep time when our lived experience is so limited?

Can physical intimacy with ancient materials create a more meaningful understanding than intellectual study?

What does it mean to transform scientific artifacts into objects of pleasure?

Is humor an appropriate—or perhaps necessary—tool for grappling with the incomprehensible vastness of geological history?

Final Reflection

The Asstral Traveler is absurd, provocative, and oddly poetic. It uses shock value not merely for attention, but to make us reconsider how we relate to time, to our bodies, and to the extinct life that preceded us. In an era where we're increasingly disconnected from both our physical selves and the deep history of our planet, perhaps such radical interventions are necessary.

Whether you find it profound, ridiculous, or both, the work succeeds in making the abstract concept of 140 million years suddenly, startlingly tangible.

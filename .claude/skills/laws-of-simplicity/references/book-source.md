# The Laws of Simplicity — Source Material

Curated passages and canonical quotes from John Maeda, *The Laws of Simplicity*, MIT Press, 2006 (OCR'd from the publicly distributed PDF). Use this as the authoritative reference when the user wants Maeda's actual framing, an original quote, or a deeper read on a specific law.

## Master Definition

> **"Simplicity is about subtracting the obvious, and adding the meaningful."** — Law 10, THE ONE

Maeda also calls THE ONE the single law to remember if you only remember one.

> **"Technology has made our lives more full, yet at the same time we've become uncomfortably 'full.'"** — opening line

> **"Technology and life only become complex if you let it be so."** — closing essay (LIFE)

## The Three Clusters

Maeda groups the 10 Laws into three flavors of simplicity, increasing in subtlety:

- **Basic simplicity (1–3)**: Reduce, Organize, Time. Immediately applicable to product/room/screen layout.
- **Intermediate simplicity (4–6)**: Learn, Differences, Context. More subtle in meaning.
- **Deep simplicity (7–9)**: Emotion, Trust, Failure. "Still ripening on the vine."
- **Law 10 (The One)** sums up the entire set.

The Three Keys (Away, Open, Power) sit alongside the Laws as technology-domain markers — "areas in which to invest R&D resources, or simply to keep an eye on."

## Law 1 — REDUCE

> *The simplest way to achieve simplicity is through thoughtful reduction.*

Core tension: **HOW SIMPLE CAN YOU MAKE IT? ↔ HOW COMPLEX DOES IT HAVE TO BE?**

> "When in doubt, just remove. But be careful of what you remove."

When functional reduction has hit its limit, apply **SHE**:

- **SHRINK** — small, light, thin objects lower expectations and earn forgiveness. "The smaller the object, the more forgiving we can be when it misbehaves." (iPod thinness, ThinkPad clamshell tapering to nothing.)
- **HIDE** — bury complexity behind doors, menus, click-to-reveal. "If the deceit feels less like malevolence, more like magic, then hidden complexities become more of a treat than a nuisance." (Swiss army knife, clamshell phone, computer menu bar.)
- **EMBODY** — invest the smaller/hidden product with perceived or actual quality so it doesn't read as cheap. "Quality can be actual, as embodied by better materials and craftsmanship; or the quality can be perceived, as portrayed in a thoughtful marketing campaign." (Bang & Olufsen weighting their remote heavier than expected to convey quality. "3 CCDs" sticker that has to violate HIDE because the value would otherwise be invisible.)

> "SHE SHE'D. Lessen what you can and conceal everything else without losing the sense of inherent value."

## Law 2 — ORGANIZE

> *Organization makes a system of many appear fewer.*

The key reframe: don't just hide — **integrate**. Ask "What goes with what?"

**SLIP** is Maeda's ad hoc grouping process:

- **SORT** — write each item on a post-it; physically move them around to find natural groupings.
- **LABEL** — name each group. If no name comes, assign an arbitrary code (letter, number, color).
- **INTEGRATE** — merge groups that look alike. "In general, the fewer the groups the better."
- **PRIORITIZE** — apply Pareto's 80/20: focus the highest-priority 20% into a single set that gets the most attention. "Everything is important, but knowing where to start is the critical first step."

**Tabs (the keyboard tab key)** are Maeda's favorite "rare sort of visual magic that always works" — "the one key on the keyboard that is designed to make information simpler."

**The Gestalt principle**: the mind groups, simplifies, and "fills in the blanks." iPod evolution illustrates this: original (buttons around dial) → middle version (separated buttons, looked complex) → final (everything integrated into one wheel — "blurred" into a single gestalt). Maeda renders the final one as a **blur** of dots in his diagrams.

> "The best designers in the world all squint when they look at something. They squint to see the forest from the trees—to find the right balance. Squint at the world. You will see more, by seeing less."

## Law 3 — TIME

> *Savings in time feel like simplicity.*

Core tension: **HOW CAN YOU MAKE THE WAIT SHORTER? ↔ HOW CAN YOU MAKE THE WAIT MORE TOLERABLE?**

Two strategies — apply SHE again:

- **SHRINK time** — actually make it faster. (FedEx, McDonald's, RFID inventory, Apple's iPod Shuffle removing the act of choosing.)
- **HIDE/EMBODY time** — when you can't shrink it, change perception:
  - Casinos remove all clocks and windows.
  - **Progress bars**: in Apple's experiment, users *perceived* tasks as completing faster when shown a progress bar than with no bar at all. A smooth, gradually-filling bar (right-side example) feels shorter than a chunky stepwise one (left-side example).
  - Whole Foods hands out free cookies during the long Thanksgiving checkout line. Care during waiting beats no care at all.
  - Crosswalk countdown timers, "you have N minutes" hold-message voice prompts.

> "Knowledge is comfort, and comfort lies at the heart of simplicity."

Raymond Loewy's **streamlining** is also called out: styling to *imply* speed (1930s aerodynamic toaster, Coca-Cola bottle, Alienware "hotrod" aesthetics).

## Law 4 — LEARN

> *Knowledge makes everything simpler.*

Two big mnemonics:

**BRAIN** — Maeda's mantra for teaching:
- **B**asics are the beginning.
- **R**epeat yourself often.
- **A**void creating desperation. ("Wow" easily becomes "woah.")
- **I**nspire with examples.
- **N**ever forget to repeat yourself.

**RELATE–TRANSLATE–SURPRISE!** — Maeda's design teaching shortcut:
- **Relate** to something the user already knows (the desktop metaphor mapping to file folders).
- **Translate** that relationship into the new context.
- **Surprise** with an unexpected, delightful payoff that makes the leap worthwhile (digital files can be searched, copied, redistributed in ways physical files can't).

The "engineering approach" is RTFM ("Read The F*cking Manual"). The "designer approach" is RELATE-TRANSLATE-SURPRISE. Italian design tends to invert: SURPRISE-TRANSLATE-RELATE (Studio65's lip-shaped sofa).

> "Difficult tasks seem easier when they are 'need to know' rather than 'nice to know.'"

## Law 5 — DIFFERENCES

> *Simplicity and complexity need each other.*

Without complexity to contrast against, simplicity is invisible.

> "The more complexity there is in the market, the more that something simpler stands out."

The key concept: **rhythm**. A graph oscillating up to complexity, down to simplicity. "Variety tends to keep our attention when the rhythm of difference captivates."

Maeda phonetically describes a jazzy rhythm: *"Taa taa ti ti taa. Rest. Ti ti ti ti taa taa…"* A monotonic *"taa taa taa taa taa…"* loses the audience by the last beat. Pure unrelenting simplicity is just as monotonous as pure unrelenting complexity.

The Mori Hiroaki sheep-illustration business card (the one card on his desk that won't get thrown out) illustrates how a single unique element gains power because everything else is uniform.

---

## Law 6 — CONTEXT

> *What lies in the periphery of simplicity is definitely not peripheral.*

> "Nothing is something."

White space, emptiness, ambient surroundings — these *are* the design, not the absence of it. (Japanese shrine cordoned off with rope around an empty rectangle. Tea-room ambience changes the taste of the food.)

Core tension: **HOW DIRECTED CAN I STAND TO FEEL? ↔ HOW DIRECTIONLESS CAN I AFFORD TO BE?** — the "comfortably lost" tradeoff. Trail markers that pop into focus when needed, then recede.

> "More white space means that less information is presented. In turn, proportionately more attention shall be paid to that which is made less available. When there is less, we appreciate everything much more."

Negroponte to Maeda: "Become a light bulb instead of a laser beam." (Illuminate everything around, not just the focal point.)

---

## Law 7 — EMOTION

> *More emotions are better than less.*

Counterweight to Law 1. "When emotions are considered above everything else, don't be afraid to add more ornament or layers of meaning."

> **"Form follows function" gives way to "Feeling follows form."**

Maeda's rule for adding the *right* kind of more: **"Feel, and feel for."** Start by being aware of your own feelings, then empathize with the user.

**Aichaku (愛着)** — Japanese term, "love-fit." A deep emotional attachment to an object: "love for an object that deserves affection not for what it does, but for what it is." Animism: the bento-box surfaces are *alive*. AIBO, Tamagotchi, Neopets all exploit the same human urge.

**Nude electronics** vs. **carrying cases**: phones got cold and skinny (SHE-d). Accessories warm them back up. "The combination of a simple object together with a host of optional accessories gives consumers the benefit of expressing their feelings and feelings for their objects."

> **ROE — Return on Emotion.** "A certain kind of more is always better than less—more care, more love, and more meaningful actions."

The Italian oncologist + the brave dying woman: pure clarity (10-min appointments) lacked comfort. She founded centers near oncology units with intensely artful, beautifully designed spaces. Art tempered with design.

## Law 8 — TRUST

> *In simplicity we trust.*

Core tension: **HOW MUCH DO YOU NEED TO KNOW ABOUT A SYSTEM? ↔ HOW MUCH DOES THE SYSTEM KNOW ABOUT YOU?**

Two opposite paths to trust:

- **LEAN BACK** — surrender to a Master. Bang & Olufsen "doesn't focus on the quality of sound, but on the quality of *leaning back*." Sushi *omakase* ("I leave it up to you"): no menu, the chef decides. Trust is ingrained from infancy when a parent meets every need.
- **UNDO** — relationship of convenience, no Master needed. Returnable purchases. Cmd-Z. "Power is equally balanced between experience and user such that neither side has the upper hand." "Put the UNDO button away when dealing with real people if possible."

Lean back demands trust paid back consistently; undo demands no trust at all. Both produce simplicity.

> "Knowing that a purchase is correctable later makes the shopping process simpler because you know that any decision made is not final."

---

## Law 9 — FAILURE

> *Some things can never be made simple.*

> "There's an ROF (Return On Failure) when you try to simplify—which is to learn from your mistakes."

Failure as honesty: complexity and simplicity need each other (Law 5), so eradicating complexity entirely would destroy simplicity itself. "Failing to achieve simplicity is an important service to humanity."

Maeda lists his book's own flaws: "ACRONYM OVERLOAD" (SHE, SLIP, BRAIN, SHE again…), "BAD GESTALTS" (laws get more ambiguous as the book progresses), "TOO MANY LAWS" — which sets up Law 10.

---

## Law 10 — THE ONE

> *Simplicity is about subtracting the obvious, and adding the meaningful.*

The single law to keep if you keep nothing else.

The Japan National Rugby Team coach Jean-Pierre Ellisalde's image: the team had become predictable; he urged them to "become like the bubbles in a glass of champagne, floating upward in unexpected and elegantly fluid ways." Simplicity has to operate by intuition, not just intellect.

> "Ten laws (10: one, zero), remove none (0: zero), and you're left with one (10: one)."

---

## Key 1 — AWAY

> *More appears like less by simply moving it far, far away.*

Software-as-a-service, cloud computing, remote APIs. Google's lightweight text input box gives access to a vast network you don't have to host. "An experience is made simpler by keeping the result local, and moving the actual work to a far AWAY location." Demands reliable communication with the outsourced task.

---

## Key 2 — OPEN

> *Openness simplifies complexity.*

Open source: with closed Windows, only Microsoft can fix it; with open Linux, thousands of net experts can. "The power of the many can outweigh the power of the few."

APIs are a softer middle ground: expose functionality, keep source private (Amazon API, Google Maps API). "For free" can lead to "for fee" (Ruby on Rails free, 37signals' related services paid).

Rooted in TRUST (Law 8): "If conventional capitalism is your compass, and to hear 'trust me' translates to 'f*ck you,' then you will likely choose the closed approach."

---

## Key 3 — POWER

> *Use less, gain more.*

Battery anxiety, sustainability. MIT Media Lab's self-powered wireless switch (key fob using only the energy of the button push). "Electronic devices can never be truly simple unless they are freed from their dependence on power."

Maeda's "sustainable computing" game of chicken: how long can he go on a trip without bringing the laptop power cord. "With more constraints, better solutions are revealed." Use less energy → personal sacrifice that's a philanthropic act.

---

## LIFE — Closing Essay

> *Technology and life only become complex if you let it be so.*

Ivan Illich on disabling professions: lawyers, doctors, dictionary.com — each new convenience makes us *less* capable in some way. PowerPoint sometimes fails; Maeda finds he presents better without it.

The student Marc volunteering at end-of-life shelters: each patient had a single shelf of belongings. "What are the few precious things that you can afford to keep at the end of your life when you already have so little?" A ring, a photograph, a memento. "Memories are all that matter in the end."
